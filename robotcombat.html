Bissan Bersa

30-Pound Shuffling Shell Spinner

Key Design Decisions

Shell Weapon Type: We chose a shell spinner mainly for its balance of offense and defense. It allows for 360-degree weapon coverage which protects the robot from any angle, while the comparatively large mass delivers strong kinetic energy transfer. Additionally the spinning shell provides gyroscopic stabilization, reducing the risk of flipping when taking hits from other robots.

Shuffler Drive System: The V1 design utilized two wheel drive with skids for stabilization, but during competition we encountered substantial issues with floor inconsistencies impacting the skid performance. For V2 we solved this by replacing the wheels with shuffler drive modules. This removes the need for skids as each shuffler module has at least two points of contact with the ground and there is an added benefit of a 1.5x weight bonus for "non-traditional locomotion".

Engineering Challenges

Shuffler Module Design: Building a shuffler module is much more difficult than just mating a motor shaft to a wheel hub. We needed to design a cam shaft to convert the rotational motion from the motor into an oscillating walking motion while taking into account the desired speed and torque requirements. In the end, we decided on 4 feet for the combination of stability, smooth shuffling, and weight. We were also able to come up with a clever cam disc which could be used in all 4 positions to greatly simplify assembly.

Shell Manufacturing: One of the main reasons that shell spinners are not very common in combat robotics is that it can be very difficult and expensive to manufacture. Since we decided to build this robot after graduating, we no longer had access to Georgia Tech's manufacturing facilities or funds which magnified these issues. Our solution was to modify an existing object that was already shell-shaped: a car wheel. This not only provided us with a cost-effective weapon, but also clear design constraints since the chosen wheel had fixed dimensions and mass. All we needed to do was cut off some unnecessary sections on the top and bottom of the wheel and weld on pieces of square tubing to act as teeth for improved energy transfer.
